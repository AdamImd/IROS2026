\begin{abstract}
Robotic manipulation in human-centered and cluttered environments requires not only dexterous control, but also the ability to sense and regulate contact forces across the entire arm. Fingertip and hand sensing alone are insufficient, as whole-arm tactile sensing is needed to embrace or avoid contact during manipulation. However, building effective tactile skins remains challenging due to trade-offs in fabrication cost, surface coverage, taxel density, sensing range beyond binary contact detection, and long-term durability and repeatability.
In this work, we present two sensor architectures for manufacturing force-sensing skins that are low-cost, high-density, easy to fabricate, and compatible with commercially available robot platforms, differing in their force sensing range, flexibility, and repeatability to suit diverse mobile manipulation applications. The first is a textile-based sensor offering flexibility and conformability to complex arm geometries; the second is an additive-manufacturing-based sensor constructed from 3D-printed components that conform to the robot arm's geometry for a more rigid and durable solution. We describe the key design choices that balance cost, coverage, and sensitivity, demonstrate these sensors across a range of mobile manipulation scenarios, and discuss how they can serve as building blocks for a whole-body tactile skin suite adaptable to real-world applications.
For more details and results, refer to the project website: https://anonymous-8169.github.io/

\end{abstract}
