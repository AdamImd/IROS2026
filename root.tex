\begin{abstract}
Robotic manipulation in human-centered and cluttered environments requires not only dexterous control, but also the ability to sense and regulate contact forces across the entire arm. Fingertip and hand sensing alone are insufficient, as whole-arm tactile sensing is needed to embrace or avoid contact during manipulation. However, building effective tactile skins remains challenging due to trade-offs in fabrication cost, surface coverage, taxel density, sensing range beyond binary contact detection, and long-term durability and repeatability.
In this work, we present two complementary force-sensing skin designs that are low-cost, high-density, easy to fabricate, and compatible with commercially available robot platforms, differing in their force-sensitive range and repeatability to suit diverse mobile manipulation applications. The first is a fabric-based sensor using conductive fabric layers with velostat; the second is a TPU-based sensor constructed from 3D-printed components conforming to the robot arm's geometry, using Velostat and copper tape. We describe the key design choices that balance cost, coverage, and sensitivity, demonstrate these sensors across a range of mobile manipulation tasks, and discuss how they can serve as building blocks for a whole-body tactile skin suite tailored to real-world applications.
