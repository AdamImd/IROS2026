\begin{abstract}
Robotic manipulation in human-centered and cluttered environments requires not only dexterous control but also the ability to sense and regulate contact forces across the entire arm. Fingertip and hand sensing alone are insufficient, as whole-arm tactile sensing is needed to embrace or avoid contact during manipulation. Yet effective tactile skins remain challenging due to trade-offs in fabrication cost, surface coverage, taxel density, sensing range beyond binary contact detection, and long-term durability and repeatability.
We present two complementary force-sensing skin architectures that are low-cost, easy to fabricate, and customizable to fit various robotic arms and applications. The first is a textile-based sensor that conforms to complex arm geometries; the second is an additive-manufacturing-based sensor built from 3D-printed components that conform to the arm's geometry for alignment and sensing higher forces. For both designs, we outline manufacturing methods and tunable parameters such as material stiffness, layer thickness, spacing, and ridge geometry to adjust operating range and hysteresis. We characterize operating range and repeatability and demonstrate the sensors in real-world scenarios.
For more details and results, refer to the project website: https://anonymous-8169.github.io/

\end{abstract}
