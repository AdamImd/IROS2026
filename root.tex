\begin{abstract}
Robotic manipulation in human-centered, cluttered environments benefits from sensing and regulating contact forces across the entire arm, but tactile skins still face trade-offs in cost, coverage, taxel density, force range beyond binary contact, and long-term durability and repeatability.
We present two complementary force-sensing skin architectures that are low-cost, easy to fabricate, and compatible with commercially available robot platforms: a textile-based sensor that conforms to complex arm geometries and an additive-manufacturing-based sensor built from 3D-printed components for durable alignment. For both designs, we outline tunable parameters (material stiffness, layer thickness, spacing, ridge geometry) that adjust operating range and hysteresis. We characterize operating range and repeatability, and demonstrate the sensors in contact avoidance and contact embracing on a mobile manipulator.
For more details and results, refer to the project website: https://anonymous-8169.github.io/

\end{abstract}
