In this work, we presented two complementary whole-arm tactile skin architectures designed to overcome the prevailing trade-offs between fabrication complexity, cost, and sensing performance in mobile manipulation. By leveraging textile and additive manufacturing (AM) processes, we demonstrated that high-coverage force-sensing skins can be realized for under \$10 per link using accessible materials like Velostat and TPU.

Our characterization revealed distinct operational profiles for each design: the textile-based sensor, with its low detection threshold and high flexibility, is uniquely suited for contact avoidance and high-sensitivity human-robot interaction. In contrast, the AM-based sensor's rigid-conformal shell and "normally open" taxel resistance significantly reduce cross-talk and provide the durability required for contact-embracing tasks, such as bracing against surfaces or modulating high-force interactions. Real-world experiments on a mobile manipulator validated these designs, showing that integrating such tactile feedback into whole-body inverse kinematics enables robots to move beyond simple collision avoidance toward deliberate and safe environmental exploitation.

Despite these gains, several technical challenges remain for future investigation. The current taxel matrix scales roughly $2\sqrt{N_{\text{taxels}}}$ in wiring complexity, and rotary axis make external wiring difficult. Future work in embedded local processing and wireless communication may be able to eliminate wiring issues at scale. Additionally, while our power-law model effectively captures force-resistance mappings, more sophisticated compensation for hysteresis—particularly in the viscoelastic TPU shells of the AM design—and temperature-induced drift will be essential for high-precision tasks. Finally, future iterations will explore and characterize the design and material variables for both sensors. By providing open fabrication guidelines and a rigorous quantitative baseline, this work establishes a practical foundation for the widespread adoption of whole-body tactile awareness in complex, human-centered environments.
