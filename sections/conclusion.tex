\section{Conclusion}
In this work, we present two complementary whole-arm tactile skin designs for mobile manipulators that balance cost, coverage, and sensitivity. The fabric-based sensor provides high sensitivity and a low detection threshold, making it suitable for contact avoidance and early detection of incidental contact. The TPU-based sensor offers a broader force-sensitive range and enhanced durability, making it ideal for contact embracing tasks and higher load interactions. Through characterization and real-world demonstrations, we show that these sensors enable safer and more effective manipulation strategies that leverage controlled environmental contacts rather than avoiding them.


Several limitations suggest directions for future work. While the current sensor design provides a practical solution, contact near ridges may produce non-uniform force readings due to the more rigid structure. Future iterations could explore alternative ridge geometries, compliant sublayers, or graded materials to improve consistency across the surface. While our sensor provides analog force measurements, we have not yet explored hysteresis, drift, or non-linearity characteristics in detail, as we only fit a simple curve to the loading response. More sophisticated calibration methods, temperature compensation, or learning-based estimators could improve force estimation accuracy and long-term stability.

While this work provides a simple assembly process, the wiring complexity increases roughly with the square root of the number of taxels. Future designs could investigate on-board microcontrollers, multiplexed buses, or modular connectors to reduce harnessing and simplify installation on different robot platforms. To further streamline fabrication, fully 3D printed conductive materials could eliminate the need for manual assembly of discrete peiezoresistive sensor components and enable printing the entire skin in a single step.

Looking forward, we see several promising research directions. Integrating the tactile skin with learning-based policies could enable robots to discover contact-rich behaviors that improve robustness in clutter. Extending the framework to whole-body control for humanoid or service robots could enable safer physical human-robot interaction beyond traditional bimanual manipulation. Finally, benchmarking across tasks and platforms would help quantify how sensing density, coverage, and force range affect downstream manipulation performance.

In summary, this work demonstrates that combining accessible whole-arm tactile sensing with contact-aware kinematic optimization enables mobile manipulators to safely and effectively exploit environmental contacts during manipulation. By addressing both hardware and algorithmic challenges, we take a step toward practical, scalable tactile skins that let robots interact with their surroundings more fluidly and adaptively.
