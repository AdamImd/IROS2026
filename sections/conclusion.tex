In this work, we presented two complementary whole-arm tactile skin architectures designed to address trade-offs between fabrication complexity, cost, and sensing performance in mobile manipulation. By leveraging textile and additive manufacturing (AM) processes, we demonstrated force-sensing skins with taxels spaced 20~mm (2~cm) center-to-center and material cost under \$10 per link using Velostat, conductive fabric, copper tape, and TPU.

Our characterization revealed distinct operational profiles for each design: the textile-based sensor, with its lower detection threshold and flexible sleeve, is configured for contact avoidance and light-touch human-robot interaction. In contrast, the AM-based sensor's rigid-conformal shell and "normally open" taxel resistance are configured for contact-embracing tasks, such as bracing against surfaces or modulating higher-force interactions. Real-world experiments on a mobile manipulator validated these designs in contact avoidance and contact embracing behaviors, showing that tactile feedback can be integrated into whole-body inverse kinematics for contact-aware motion.

Despite these gains, specific technical challenges remain for future investigation. The current taxel matrix scales roughly $2\sqrt{N_{\text{taxels}}}$ in wiring complexity, and rotary axes make external wiring difficult. Future work in embedded local processing and wireless communication could reduce wiring issues at scale. Additionally, while our power-law model captures force-resistance mappings, compensation for hysteresis—particularly in the viscoelastic TPU shells of the AM design—and temperature-induced drift will be important for tasks requiring tighter force-estimation error. Finally, future iterations will explore and characterize the design and material variables for both sensors. By providing open fabrication guidelines and quantitative characterization, this work establishes a baseline for whole-body tactile awareness in contact-rich mobile manipulation.
