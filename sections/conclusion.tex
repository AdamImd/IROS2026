\section{Conclusion}
We present two complementary whole-arm tactile skin designs for mobile manipulators that balance cost, coverage, and sensitivity. The textile sensor provides high sensitivity and a low detection threshold for contact avoidance and early contact detection, while the AM sensor offers broader force range and higher durability for contact-embracing tasks. Characterization and real-world demonstrations show that these sensors support safer, more effective manipulation that uses controlled environmental contact rather than strictly avoiding it.

Key limitations remain: ridge-contact non-uniformity, limited treatment of hysteresis/drift/nonlinearity, and wiring complexity that scales with taxel count. Future work includes improved mechanical designs (e.g., ridge geometry or compliant layers), stronger calibration methods (including temperature compensation or learning-based estimation), and simplified electronics integration via multiplexing, modular connectors, or local embedded sensing.

Overall, this work takes a practical step toward scalable tactile skins by combining accessible fabrication with contact-aware control, and provides a foundation for broader benchmarking and whole-body deployment.
