\section{Introduction}
Humans naturally exploit whole-arm contact during manipulation, using surfaces to brace, stabilize, or guide movements in cluttered environments. Recent advances in robotic tactile sensing have demonstrated the value of extending sensing beyond grippers to enable safer physical human-robot interaction~\cite{10610014}, whole-body contact awareness~\cite{8812712,kohlbrenner2025gentact}, contact-rich manipulation~\cite{8593555,10.7551/mitpress/9481.003.0014}, and force-feedback teleoperation~\cite{imdieke2025spark}. Despite these advances, widespread adoption remains limited: most robotic systems still lack tactile coverage beyond the hands, limiting their ability to detect unexpected arm contact, maintain contact-force targets, or use environmental contacts during manipulation~\cite{6502183,Cyskin}.

Several challenges continue to hinder the broader adoption of whole-arm or whole-body tactile sensing in robotics. Existing research prototypes that extend tactile sensing to the forearm or whole arm~\cite{6502183,Cyskin,kohlbrenner2024machine} often rely on custom PCBs, proprietary sensor modules, or specialized fabrication processes, creating a gap between demonstration systems and reproducible deployment.

Table~\ref{tab:related_work_comparison} compares the sensing layouts, reported measurements, and manufacturing requirements of representative systems. The reported sensing layout and measurement preserve each work's native reporting convention while distinguishing calibrated force or pressure from relative magnitude signals, contact localization, and binary contact.

To bridge these gaps, a tactile skin must satisfy both sensing and deployment requirements: it must detect the contact forces relevant to manipulation, produce repeatable readings under repeated loading, and be manufacturable for existing robot links without permanent modification. We therefore pursued the research question of \textit{which measurable properties make tactile skins useful for mobile manipulators, and how can those skins be fabricated using textile and additive manufacturing (AM) methods?} Through iterative design, we evaluate three factors for whole-arm or body sensing: (1) \textbf{operating force range} - the range of forces a sensor can distinctly sense, (2) \textbf{sensing repeatability} - the consistency of sensor measurements across repeated loading and unloading cycles, (3) \textbf{deployability} - fabrication and installation requirements on commercially available robots without permanent link modification.

We propose two complementary force-sensing skin architectures, shown mounted on the Spot arm in Fig.~\ref{fig:teaser}. Both use taxels arranged on a 20~mm grid, cost under \$10 per link in materials, and can be fabricated by ironing fabric layers or printing flexible shells. The resulting designs differ in force-sensing range, flexibility, and repeatability, so we configure them for two mobile manipulation behaviors: contact avoidance and contact embracing. The textile sensor sandwiches a piezoresistive material between conductive fabric layers, whereas the additive-manufacturing-based sensor uses 3D-printed components shaped from the arm's mesh to form a conformal pressure-sensing matrix of conductive layers separated by a piezoresistive layer.

They differ in their force-sensitive range and repeatability, covering a wide spectrum of forces and suitable for diverse mobile manipulation applications. The first is a textile-based sensor using conductive fabric layers with a piezoresistive material in between; the second is a AM-based sensor constructed using~\cite{FSR_WTL} with 3D-printed or textile inner and outer shells that define a high-coverage pressure-sensing matrix formed by conductive layers (copper tape) separated by a piezoresistive layer, generated directly from the arm's 3D mesh to conform to complex geometries.



The contributions of this paper are as follows:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item Two complementary sensor architectures for whole-arm force-sensing skins, instantiated as a textile-based and an additive-manufacturing-based design, each with taxels spaced 20~mm center-to-center, material cost under \$10 per link, and removable mounting on a commercially available mobile manipulator.
\item Fabrication guidelines and sensor characterization that demonstrate the practical feasibility, force-sensing range, and repeatability of each design, along with the key design parameters that can be tuned to meet application-specific requirements.
\item Experimental validation on a mobile manipulator platform demonstrating real-world sensing performance across contact-avoidance and contact-embracing scenarios.
\end{itemize}
