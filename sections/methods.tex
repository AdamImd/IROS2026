\section{Sensor Design}
We propose two whole-arm tactile skin sensors for mobile manipulators that balance three practical requirements: (1) operating force range, (2) sensing repeatability under repeated loading/unloading, and (3) deployability on commercial robots with minimal changes to existing robot hardware. Both sensors are optimized for maximal surface coverage, analog force readings across individual taxels, and low-cost, accessible fabrication. They share a common operating principle: a matrix of conductive rows and columns separated by a piezoresistive layer (Velostat). When contact is made at a taxel, the piezoresistive layer compresses, producing a change in resistance that is read by a microcontroller via a voltage divider circuit. While the designs are adaptable to most robot geometries, we target the Boston Dynamics Spot arm as our primary platform for design and evaluation.

We implement two complementary variants: (1) a fabric-based sensor using conductive fabric layers, and (2) a TPU-based sensor with 3D-printed conformal shells and copper-tape electrodes. The fabric sensor prioritizes flexibility, rapid installation, and sensitivity at lower force ranges, while the TPU sensor provides a more robust and durable solution suited to higher force ranges. Together they tile the force sensing range needed for diverse mobile manipulation tasks. We describe the design and fabrication of each in the following sections, with an overview shown in Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}.

\subsection{Fabric Sensor}
The fabric sensor shown in Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both} is designed as a flexible and sensitive alternative to the TPU sensor, conforming naturally to the robot arm without rigid shells. Like the TPU sensor, it uses a matrix of conductive rows and columns separated by a piezoresistive Velostat layer, with the same taxel pitch of approximately \textit{20mm}. The sensor is formed into a cylindrical sleeve using velcro, allowing it to be easily wrapped around and removed from the robot link for maintenance. Rows and columns are interfaced with the microcontroller via snap buttons, with a~\textit{47$\Omega$} bias resistor used in the voltage divider circuit during characterization.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Inner layer:} The inner layer template is derived from a 2D projection of the robot link geometry and cut from \ktxt{TODO}, chosen for its flexibility and high friction to prevent slipping against the robot surface. Rows of conductive fabric are adhered to the inner layer, with excess fabric removed at taxel boundaries to electrically isolate adjacent rows.
\item \textbf{Outer layer:} The outer layer uses the same template but is cut from \ktxt{TODO}, providing a softer contact surface for improved sensitivity. Columns of conductive fabric are adhered to the outer layer, with excess fabric removed at taxel boundaries to isolate adjacent columns.
\item \textbf{Force Sensitive Layer:} Velostat squares are cut such that they are slightly wider than the conductive rows and columns to prevent short-circuiting. 
\item \textbf{Assembly:} The inner layer, Velostat squares, and outer layer are stacked in order and a fabric adhesive is used around each Velostat square to secure it. The sensor is then wrapped around the robot link, then secured with hook-and-loop tape. Snap buttons connect wires to each row and column, which are routed to a microcontroller.
\end{enumerate}
The average material cost for this sensor is approximately \textit{less than \$\ktxt{TODO}} per link, making it a highly accessible option for large-area deployment.

\subsection{TPU Sensor}
To design the 3D-printed skin, we start from the link-level mesh files provided by Boston Dynamics (Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-2, yellow) as part of their URDF models. For each link, we apply the following sequence of CAD operations:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Inner layer:} Generate a conformal shell with a uniform thickness of \textit{3mm} offset outward from the robot's base geometry (Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-2, green).
\item \textbf{Outer layer:} Generate a second shell with a wall thickness of \textit{3mm}, whose inner surface is offset by \textit{5mm} from the robot's base geometry, leaving a gap between the two layers to accommodate the sensing stack (Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-2, orange).
\item \textbf{Ridges:} Within the inter-shell region, add horizontal ridges on the inner shell and vertical ridges on the outer shell, each \textit{2mm} thick. The spacing between adjacent ridges determines the taxel density (Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-2, cutout).
\item \textbf{Clearance cuts:} Material is removed near kinematic joints to prevent interference during motion, and a longitudinal cut is added along each shell to facilitate installation on the robot arm (Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-1).
\end{enumerate}


Both shells are printed in 95A TPU with a ridge spacing of approximately \textit{20mm}. To assemble the sensing stack, a Velostat sheet is laminated with copper tape on one side using the tape's conductive adhesive, and a double-sided adhesive layer is applied to attach the laminate to the inner shell. Copper tape is applied directly to the outer shell to form the opposing electrodes. This layered structure is shown in Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-5. Wires are connected to each row and column and routed to a microcontroller. The outer shell is then snapped onto the inner shell and secured to the robot arm.

If binary contact detection is sufficient for a given application, the Velostat layer can be omitted and copper tape adhered directly to both shells, significantly reducing cost and assembly complexity. In either configuration, the average material cost remains under \textit{\$10} per link, making this sensor well-suited for large-area deployment.

We model our sensor as a grid of taxels, where each taxel provides an analog reading corresponding to the magnitude of contact force at that location. The taxels are arranged in a grid pattern, with rows and columns defined by the conductive materials on the inner and outer layers of the sensor. We calibrate the sensor by applying known forces at specific taxel locations and recording the corresponding sensor readings, allowing us to create a model, mapping sensor readings to contact forces. 

To measure the resistance of each taxel, we use a voltage divider circuit, where the taxel is connected in series with a known bias resistor. The voltage across the bias resistor is measured, and we first use Ohm's law to calculate the resistance $R$ of the taxel based on the measured voltage $V_\text{out}$, the input voltage $V_\text{in}$, and the known bias resistor value $R_\text{bias}$:
\begin{equation}
R = \left( \frac{V_\text{in}-V_\text{out}}{V_\text{out}} \right) R_\text{bias} 
\end{equation}


Because the measured resistance includes a base resistance in series with the force-sensitive resistance, we write

\begin{equation}
R = R_\text{base} + R_\text{FSR}.
\end{equation}

We use a power law to model the FSR,

\begin{equation}
R = R_\text{base} + A F^{B}.
\end{equation}






We then fit a power law with a base resistance $R_\text{base}$ in series with the force sensitive resistance $R_\text{FSR}$, where the force $F$ is related to the taxel resistance as follows:

\begin{equation}
F=\left(\frac{R-R_\text{base}}{A}\right)^{\frac{1}{B}}
\end{equation}

where $A$, $B$, and $R_\text{base}$ are the parameters of the power law, which are determined through calibration and curve fitting. This model allows us to estimate the contact force at each taxel based on the measured resistance, enabling the sensor to provide quantitative force information across its surface.

We define the sensitive range of each sensor as the force interval from its detection threshold (minimum detectable force) to saturation (maximum force for which changes in resistance remain informative). Fig.~\ref{fig:overall_comparison} summarizes the resulting coverage across sensor variants.

\subsubsection{Minimum Detectable Force}
The \emph{sensitive range} is the force interval from the minimum detectable force (detection threshold) to the saturation force (largest force that still produces informative signal change). In our pipeline, the detection threshold is measured per taxel as the \emph{break force}:
\[
F_{\mathrm{det}}=\min\{F>0:\; R(F)\le R_{\mathrm{thr}}\},\qquad R_{\mathrm{thr}}=4000~\Omega,
\]

\subsubsection{Saturation Force}
The saturation force is measured from the fitted response $R(F)=AF^B+C$ by mapping it through the expected voltage-divider readout
\[
V(F)=V_{\mathrm{in}}\frac{R(F)}{R(F)+R_{\mathrm{ref}}},\qquad V_{\mathrm{in}}=5~\mathrm{V},
\]
and defining
\[
F_{\mathrm{sat}}=\max\Bigl\{F\in[F_{\mathrm{det}},30~\mathrm{N}]:\; \bigl|dV/dF\bigr|\ge 0.03~\mathrm{V/N}\Bigr\},
\]
where $R_{\mathrm{ref}}$ is the reference resistor value, which is selected over a range of reference resistors to find the one that maximizes the sensitive range, and $[F_{\min},F_{\max}]$ follows the configured force limits (e.g., $F_{\max}=30~\mathrm{N}$). The sensitive range is $[F_{\mathrm{det}},F_{\mathrm{sat}}]$: below $F_{\mathrm{det}}$ the sensor behaves like an open circuit (negligible signal), while above $F_{\mathrm{sat}}$ the response flattens and force estimates become ambiguous.




\subsection{Consistency}

\subsubsection{Repeatability}
For each taxel, we sample 10 points uniformly across $[F_{\mathrm{det}}, 30 N]$, and compute the standard deviation of the sensor readings across repeated loading cycles at each force level. We summarize single-force repeatability as the standard deviation $\sigma_R(F)$ of the sensor readings across cycles at each force level $F$:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_R(F) = \mathrm{std}\!\left(\{R_i(F)\}_{i=1}^{N_c}\right),
\end{equation}
along with the corresponding mean $\mu_R(F)$ and the number of valid cycles $N_c$.

To normalize the repeatability metric across different force levels and sensor configurations, we compute the coefficient of variation (CV) across cycles at each force level $F$:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CV}(F_j)=\frac{\sigma_R(F_j)}{\mu_R(F_j)}
\end{equation}

We average the CV across each sampled force level to obtain a single multi-force repeatability metric for each taxel:

\begin{equation}
\overline{\mathrm{CV}} = \frac{1}{N_s} \sum_{j=1}^{N_s} \mathrm{CV}(F_j)
\end{equation}

and we then report the mean $\overline{\mathrm{CV}}$ across taxels, along with the corresponding standard deviation across taxels, to summarize the overall repeatability of the sensor. This multi-force repeatability metric captures the consistency of the sensor's response across its operating range and the variability across taxels, providing a comprehensive measure of the sensor's repeatability performance.

\subsubsection{Hysteresis}
We quantify hysteresis using the discrepancy between the loading and unloading curves in the resistance domain. For each taxel, we fit power-law models to the loading and unloading data,
\begin{equation}
R(F)=AF^B+C,
\end{equation}
and invert them to obtain $F_{\text{load}}(R)$ and $F_{\text{unload}}(R)$ over the region where inversion is valid. Hysteresis is then computed as the area between the two inverted curves:
\begin{equation}
H_{\text{area}} = \int_{R_{\text{min}}}^{R_{\text{max}}} \left|F_{\text{load}}(R) - F_{\text{unload}}(R)\right| \, dR,
\end{equation}
 This area-based metric captures cumulative hysteretic behavior across the operating range, rather than at a single point.
