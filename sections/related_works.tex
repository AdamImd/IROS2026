
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_works}

Existing whole-arm and whole-body tactile sensing systems vary considerably in their force sensing range, sensing repeatability, and fabrication requirements on commercially available platforms. Early systems demonstrated the feasibility of extending tactile coverage beyond the gripper~\cite{6502183,Cyskin}, but often relied on custom PCBs or proprietary modules~\cite{6502183,Cyskin}. More recent work has improved coverage and sensing density~\cite{10610014,8812712,kohlbrenner2024machine,kohlbrenner2025gentact}, yet trade-offs remain between sensing performance and requirements such as custom electronics, knitting machines, multi-material 3D printing, or robot-specific integration. We organize our discussion of related work around these trade-offs, focusing on systems most comparable to our fabric and AM skin designs.

Compton et al.~\cite{FSR_WTL} demonstrated a textile FSR array using Eeonyx piezoresistive fabric between knit electrodes. While evaluated for on-body sensing\cite{11068986}, the design used Eeonyx fabric and was not evaluated on robotic arms. We base our textile design on this layered material approach, replacing Eeonyx with Velostat and testing construction and integration choices for robotic-arm deployment.

Similarly, RobotSweater~\cite{si2023robotsweater} provides a textile-based tactile skin for mobile manipulators with continuous force sensing and 0.36--2.0 k taxels/m$^2$ across reported prototypes, but its fabrication requires an industrial knitting machine and its characterization reports hysteresis and drift. Our fabric sensor extends this direction with a design that uses cut-and-ironed fabric layers and exposes material choices for tuning sensing response.

Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2025gentact} introduced GenTact, a tactile skin fabricated with multi-material 3D printing and conductive filaments. Its FR3 demonstration used 30 sensing nodules, and its reported reliable nodule spacing corresponds to at most 0.28 k taxels/m$^2$ under uniform spacing. Its capacitive RC-delay sensing detects binary contact at nodules rather than calibrated force magnitude. We build on their snap-on design philosophy: our AM sensor uses 3D-printed shells but incorporates a resistive matrix with 2.5 k taxels/m$^2$.

Maiolino et al.~\cite{6502183} developed a flexible large-area tactile skin, but its fabrication requires custom capacitive PCBs and supporting electronics. CySkin~\cite{Cyskin} similarly offers whole-arm tactile sensing with capacitive modules, but it is available as a proprietary package built around CySkin modules, IHB boards, and the Skinware stack. Our fabric sensor uses off-the-shelf fabric, Velostat, and snap-button wiring, with material cost under \$10 per link in our implementation.

Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2024machine} proposed a machine-learning method to localize contact in variable-density 3D tactile skins using mutual capacitance sensors embedded in silicone rubber on a 3D-printed base. Their system uses 64 mutual-capacitance intersections on a semi-conical surface and trains a contact-localization model from touch data. Our AM sensor instead uses a row-column resistive matrix, so contact localization follows directly from the active taxel without training a learned localization model.

Cheng et al.~\cite{8812712} developed an artificial whole-body skin that combines multimodal sensing with custom taxel PCBA and distributed electronics. CushSense by Xu et al.~\cite{10610014} demonstrates a soft capacitive skin with 1.1 k taxels/m$^2$ and sewing-based fabrication. In contrast, our AM sensor uses robot-link meshes to generate conforming sensor shells and achieves 2.5 k taxels/m$^2$ with material cost under \$10 per link.

Together, these works highlight a recurring trade-off: tactile skin systems with custom or proprietary electronics can provide dense sensing, while systems with simpler fabrication often reduce density, force calibration, or deployment flexibility. Our two sensors address this trade-off jointly, with the fabric sensor configured for flexible low-force contact detection and the AM sensor configured for conformal mounting and higher-threshold contact embracing.
