
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_works}

Existing whole-arm and whole-body tactile sensing systems vary considerably in their force sensing range, sensing repeatability, and ease of fabrication and deployment on commercially available platforms. Early systems demonstrated the feasibility of extending tactile coverage beyond the gripper~\ktxt{CITE?}, but relied on custom hardware that is costly or difficult to replicate~\ktxt{CITE?}. More recent work has improved coverage and sensing density~\ktxt{CITE?}, yet trade-offs between sensing performance and practical deployability remain. We organize our discussion of related work around these trade-offs, focusing on systems most comparable to our fabric and AM skin designs.

\subsection{Flexible Whole-Arm Tactile Sensors}
Compton et al.~\cite{FSR_WTL} demonstrated a textile FSR array using Eeonyx piezoresistive fabric between knit electrodes, achieving a low-cost, flexible tactile sensor. While effective for on-body sensing\cite{11068986}, the design used expensive Eeonyx fabric and lacked evaluation on robotic arms. We base our textile desing on this approach, replacing Eeonyx with the inexpensive Velostat explore variations in the design of these sensors. Our fabric sensor builds directly on their material approach but explores varaitions in construction and integration to create a more robust solution tailored for robotic applications.

Similarly, RobotSweater~\cite{si2023robotsweater} provides a low-cost, textile-based tactile skin for mobile manipulators with high coverage and continuous force sensing, but its inconsistent sensing performance limits applicability in precise manipulation tasks. Our fabric sensor extends this direction with a design that does not require a knitting machine and offers more options for tuning sensing performance.

\subsection{Static Whole-Arm Tactile Sensors}

Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2025gentact} introduced GenTact, a large-area tactile skin fabricated with 3D printing and conductive filaments, emphasizing ease of fabrication and broad coverage across robotic platforms. However, its capacitive RC-delay sensing limits sensitivity and spatial resolution, with each unit containing only six sensing nodules (36 total), which restricts force magnitude and direction estimation. We build on their snap-on design philosophy: our AM sensor uses 3D-printed shells but incorporates a resistive sensing approach to achieve higher coverage and greater sensitivity while maintaining ease of fabrication and integration.

Maiolino et al.~\cite{6502183} developed a flexible large-area tactile skin with high coverage, but its fabrication requires specialized circuitry and materials. CySkin~\cite{Cyskin} similarly offers whole-arm tactile sensing with capacitive sensors, delivering high coverage and density, but it is available only as a proprietary package, making it expensive and difficult to customize. Our fabric sensor offers a more affordable and customizable alternative that can be tailored to specific robotic platforms and applications.

Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2024machine} proposed a machine-learning method to localize contact in variable-density 3D tactile skins using mutual capacitance sensors embedded in silicone rubber on a 3D-printed base. While their results highlight the promise of learned contact models, our AM sensor delivers reliable force localization without extensive calibration or learned models, lowering the barrier to practical deployment.

Cheng et al.~\cite{8812712} developed an artificial whole-body skin that combines multimodal sensing with high coverage, but its fabrication is costly and complex. CushSense by Xu et al.~\cite{10610014} similarly demonstrates broad coverage with scalable fabrication. In contrast, our AM sensor requires only the 3D models of robot links to generate conforming sensor shells, providing high spatial resolution and sensitivity at significantly lower cost.


Together, these works highlight a consistent gap: tactile skin systems that are easy to build often trade off sensing performance, while higher-performing systems tend to be harder to fabricate or deploy. Our two sensors address this gap jointly, with the fabric sensor prioritizing flexibility and low cost, and the AM sensor prioritizing structural conformity and sensing reliability, making them complementary for whole-arm tactile sensing on mobile manipulators.
