\subsection{Rigid Whole-Arm Tactile Sensors}
Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2025gentact} introduced GenTact, a large-area tactile skin fabricated with 3D printing and conductive filaments, emphasizing ease of fabrication and broad coverage across robotic platforms. However, its capacitive RC-delay sensing limits sensitivity and spatial resolution, with each unit containing only six sensing nodules (36 total), restricting the ability to detect force magnitude and direction. Building on their snap-on design philosophy, our TPU sensor instead uses a resistive sensing approach to achieve higher spatial resolution, greater sensitivity, and broader force detection capability.

In related work, Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2024machine} proposed a machine learning method to localize contact in variable-density 3D tactile skins using mutual capacitance sensors embedded in silicone rubber on a 3D-printed base. While this demonstrates the potential of learned contact models, our TPU sensor achieves reliable force localization without requiring extensive calibration or learned models, lowering the barrier to practical deployment.

Cheng et al.~\cite{8812712} developed an artificial whole-body skin combining multimodal sensing with high coverage, but its fabrication is costly and complex. CushSense by Xu et al.~\cite{10610014} also demonstrates promising coverage and scalable fabrication. In contrast, our TPU sensor requires only the 3D models of robot links to generate conforming sensor shells, providing high spatial resolution and sensitivity at significantly lower cost.

Together, these works highlight a consistent gap: existing tactile skin systems trade off between sensing performance, fabrication accessibility, and practical deployability. Our two sensors address this gap jointly, with the fabric sensor prioritizing flexibility and low cost, and the TPU sensor prioritizing structural conformity and sensing reliability, making them complementary for whole-arm tactile sensing on mobile manipulators.
