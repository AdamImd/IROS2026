
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_works}

Existing whole-arm and whole-body tactile sensing systems vary considerably in their sensing outputs and fabrication requirements. Early systems extended tactile coverage beyond the gripper using custom capacitive PCBs or proprietary modules~\cite{6502183,Cyskin}. More recent systems use sewn capacitive taxels, custom multimodal cells, mutual-capacitance intersections, machine-knitted skins, or printed sensing nodules~\cite{10610014,8812712,kohlbrenner2024machine,si2023robotsweater,kohlbrenner2025gentact}. Table~\ref{tab:related_work_comparison} compares these systems using only quantities and capabilities explicitly reported by their authors.

Compton et al.~\cite{FSR_WTL} demonstrated a layered textile FSR by placing piezoresistive fabric between knit electrodes, and Golgouneh and Dunne~\cite{11068986} further developed a soft e-textile matrix for on-body force sensing. These works show that a row--column textile stack can provide distributed force measurements, but they target wearable sensing rather than integration on rigid robot links. Our textile sensor adapts this principle using cut Velostat, ironed conductive-fabric electrodes, snap-button connections, and a removable sleeve characterized on the target manipulation platform.

RobotSweater~\cite{si2023robotsweater} demonstrates that textile tactile skins can be deployed on mobile manipulators. Its three reported prototypes contain 16, 48, and 64 taxels and provide force-correlated tactile readings, but fabrication requires an industrial knitting machine. Our fabric sensor addresses the same whole-arm deployment problem using a cut-and-ironed construction made from sheet materials, while exposing material choices that can tune the sensing response.

Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2025gentact} introduced GenTact, a conformal tactile skin fabricated using multi-material printing and conductive filaments. Its FR3 demonstration used 30 sensing nodules across six skin units, and its RC-delay sensing reports binary contact rather than calibrated force magnitude. Our AM sensor separates structure from sensing: the printed shell provides conformal mounting and a designed normally-open gap, while copper tape and Velostat form a 20~mm-pitch resistive matrix for force estimation.

Maiolino et al.~\cite{6502183} developed a flexible large-area tactile skin using custom capacitive PCBs and supporting electronics. CySkin~\cite{Cyskin} similarly offers whole-arm tactile sensing through proprietary sensor modules, interface boards, and the Skinware stack. In contrast, our fabric sensor uses off-the-shelf fabric, Velostat, and snap-button wiring, with material cost under \$10 per link in our implementation.

Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2024machine} used 64 mutual-capacitance intersections embedded in silicone on a 3D-printed base and trained a model to localize contact on a semi-conical surface. Our AM sensor instead uses a row--column resistive matrix, so contact location follows directly from the active taxel without a learned localization model. Cheng et al.~\cite{8812712} combined force and proximity sensing using custom taxel PCBAs and distributed electronics, while CushSense~\cite{10610014} used sewn 3~cm $\times$ 3~cm capacitive taxels. Our AM design uses robot-link meshes to generate a conforming shell with a 20~mm taxel pitch and material cost under \$10 per link.

Together, these systems combine different sensing outputs, fabrication processes, electronics, and mounting strategies. Our two designs contribute accessible alternatives: a fabric sleeve configured for lower-force contact detection and an AM shell configured for conformal mounting and higher-threshold contact embracing.
