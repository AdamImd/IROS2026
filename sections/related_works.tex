
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_works}

Existing whole-arm and whole-body tactile sensing systems vary considerably in their force sensing range, sensing repeatability, and fabrication requirements on commercially available platforms. Early systems demonstrated the feasibility of extending tactile coverage beyond the gripper~\cite{6502183,Cyskin}, but often relied on custom PCBs or proprietary modules~\cite{6502183,Cyskin}. More recent systems use sewn capacitive taxels, custom multimodal cells, mutual-capacitance intersections, or printed sensing nodules~\cite{10610014,8812712,kohlbrenner2024machine,kohlbrenner2025gentact}, with different requirements for electronics, knitting machines, multi-material 3D printing, and robot-specific integration. We organize our discussion of related work around these sensing and fabrication choices, focusing on systems most comparable to our fabric and AM skin designs.

Compton et al.~\cite{FSR_WTL} demonstrated the layered textile-FSR principle by placing Eeonyx piezoresistive fabric between knit electrodes, and Golgouneh and Dunne~\cite{11068986} further developed a soft e-textile matrix for on-body force sensing. These works are important because they show that a row-column textile stack can provide soft distributed force measurements, but their target domain is wearable sensing rather than robot-link integration. For robot arms, the remaining challenges are geometric and mechanical: the sensing layers must conform to curved rigid links, maintain row-column isolation under wrapping or shell compression, and preserve repeatable force response during repeated robotic contacts. Our textile sensor uses the same row-column piezoresistive principle, but the contribution is the robot-arm implementation: replacing the Eeonyx layer with cut Velostat sheets, using ironed conductive-fabric electrodes and snap-button connections, and characterizing the resulting sleeve on the target manipulation platform.

Similarly, RobotSweater~\cite{si2023robotsweater} shows that textile tactile skins can be deployed on mobile manipulators. Its three reported prototypes contain 16, 48, and 64 taxels and provide force-correlated tactile readings. Its main limitation for replication is not sensing modality but manufacturing access: the sensor is produced on an industrial knitting machine. Our fabric sensor targets the same whole-arm deployment problem with a cut-and-ironed construction that can be made from sheet materials, while still exposing material choices for tuning sensing response.

Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2025gentact} introduced GenTact, a tactile skin fabricated with multi-material 3D printing and conductive filaments. Its FR3 demonstration used 30 sensing nodules distributed across six skin units, and the paper reports that conductive-filament resistivity limited reliable nodule spacing. Its capacitive RC-delay sensing detects binary contact at nodules rather than calibrated force magnitude. Combining GenTact's conformal geometry with the continuous force output of an e-textile matrix is therefore not direct: row-column electrodes must be routed through a curved shell without shorts, and the shell must create a repeatable compression path into the piezoresistive layer. We address this combination by separating structure from sensing: the AM shell provides conformal mounting and a designed normally-open gap, while copper tape and Velostat provide a 20~mm-pitch resistive matrix for force estimation.

Maiolino et al.~\cite{6502183} developed a flexible large-area tactile skin, but its fabrication requires custom capacitive PCBs and supporting electronics. CySkin~\cite{Cyskin} similarly offers whole-arm tactile sensing with capacitive modules, but it is available as a proprietary package built around CySkin modules, IHB boards, and the Skinware stack. Our fabric sensor uses off-the-shelf fabric, Velostat, and snap-button wiring, with material cost under \$10 per link in our implementation.

Kohlbrenner et al.~\cite{kohlbrenner2024machine} proposed a machine-learning method to localize contact in variable-density 3D tactile skins using mutual capacitance sensors embedded in silicone rubber on a 3D-printed base. Their system uses 64 mutual-capacitance intersections on a semi-conical surface and trains a contact-localization model from touch data. Our AM sensor instead uses a row-column resistive matrix, so contact localization follows directly from the active taxel without training a learned localization model.

Cheng et al.~\cite{8812712} developed an artificial whole-body skin that combines multimodal sensing with custom taxel PCBA and distributed electronics. CushSense by Xu et al.~\cite{10610014} demonstrates a soft capacitive skin with 3~cm $\times$ 3~cm taxels and sewing-based fabrication. In contrast, our AM sensor uses robot-link meshes to generate conforming sensor shells with a 20~mm taxel pitch and material cost under \$10 per link.

Together, these works use different combinations of sensing output, fabrication process, readout electronics, and mounting strategy. Our two sensors contribute two additional combinations: a fabric sleeve configured for lower-force contact detection and an AM shell configured for conformal mounting and higher-threshold contact embracing.
