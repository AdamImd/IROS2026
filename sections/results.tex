In this section, we characterize the behavior of \acro{} with the use of our Instron testing machine. We use a reference resistor of 1000$\Omega$ for the TPU sensors, and 47$\Omega$ for the fabric sensors in a voltage devider circuit. We test the response of the skin by applying a 7.5$\times$7.5mm square applicator at the center of each taxel, and measuring the voltage response of the taxel. We apply forces from \textit{0-30}N to a single taxel for 4 load-unload cycles. A power law with a bias term $F=AR^B + C$ is fitted to the loadingand unloading response of each cycle, where $F$ is the applied force and $R$ is the measured resistance. We use the fitted curves to analyze the performance of the sensor, and to estimate forces during manipulation tasks. 


\subsection{Sensitive Range}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{images/Results/overall_best_rref_comparison.pdf}
    \caption{\textbf{Criteria 1: Sensitive Range.} Comparison of the force range over which each sensor provides informative measurements, from the detection threshold (minimum detectable force) to saturation (maximum sensitive force). Larger ranges indicate broader coverage of contact forces relevant to manipulation.}
    \label{fig:overall_comparison}
\end{figure}

We define the sensitive range of each sensor as the force interval from its detection threshold (minimum detectable force) to saturation (maximum force for which changes in resistance remain informative). Fig.~\ref{fig:overall_comparison} summarizes the resulting coverage across sensor variants.

Overall, the fabric sensor provides the best performance for low-force interactions, with a near-zero detection threshold in the flat configuration and a strong response at light contacts. In contrast, the TPU sensor exhibits an elevated detection threshold, which is the force at which the sensor begins to respond. This is due to the designed gap between the inner and outer layers, which creates a binary contact behavior: as force increases, the outer layer bends and eventually makes contact with the inner layer, causing a sudden change in resistance.

When the sensor is wrapped around the arm and tightened using the velcro, we find the effective sensitive range of the Velostat-based sensors is reduced relative to the flat characterization (Fig.~\ref{fig:overall_comparison}). In our tests, mounting the fabric sensor on the arm primarily reduces the maximum force range. This may be due to the pre-compression of the sensor in the fabric sensor, which causes it to saturate at lower forces. Another potential factor is the non-uniform pressure distribution across the taxels when wrapped around a curved surface, which can cause the contact area to be restricted to small surface area. \subsection{Consistency}

\begin{figure}
    \centering

    \includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{images/Results/overall_hysteresis_area_bar.pdf}
    \caption{\textbf{Criteria 2: Consistency.} Repeatability and hysteresis metrics across sensor types. \textbf{Top:} repeatability score (deviation across repeated trials) evaluated at multiple force levels; lower is better. \textbf{Bottom:} hysteresis quantified by the area between loading and unloading curves; lower is better.}
    \label{fig:overall_consistency}
\end{figure}

We evaluate consistency using a repeatability metric that measures deviation across repeated load-unload cycles at fixed force levels, and a hysteresis metric that captures the discrepancy between loading and unloading responses (Fig.~\ref{fig:overall_consistency}).

Across the tested configurations, the fabric and TPU sensors achieve reasonably similar repeatability scores (Fig.~\ref{fig:overall_consistency}, top), indicating that both designs can provide stable measurements under repeated loading when calibrated under matched conditions (\ktxt{TODO: specify repeatability definition, e.g., std/mean at each force}).

However, the TPU sensor exhibits substantially higher hysteresis than the fabric sensor (Fig.~\ref{fig:overall_consistency}, bottom). We attribute this primarily to viscoelastic effects in the TPU shell, which cause the mechanical state of the shell to depend on the recent deformation history. In contrast, the fabric-based sensor produces more consistent loading and unloading curves, likely due to the fabric material's lower viscoelasticity and more stable contact mechanics. This suggests that while the TPU sensor can provide precise measurements, addressing hysteresis is key to collecting accurate force estimates during dynamic manipulation tasks. \ktxt{TODO: Cite WTL RNN paper}






\subsection{Real World Use Cases}

\subsection{Real World Manipulation Tasks}
We use \acro{} to perform a variety of whole body contact manipulation tasks on the Boston Dynamics Spot platform. We developed a custom contact-aware inverse kinematics algorithm, ContactIK, that incorporates tactile feedback from \acro{} to enable both contact avoidance and contact embracing behaviors during manipulation.

We demonstrate the ability of the TPU sensor to enable contact with rigid objects in the environment through a contact embracing task, where the robot uses tactile feedback to gently open a door while balancing a dustpan in the gripper. This task has a higher force range and requires the robot to actively seek contact with the door, while sensing when the door is fully open and maintaining a desired contact force. While the fabric sensor is more sensitive, it is less robust to the higher forces and high pressure points that occur during this task, and thus we use the TPU sensor for this demonstration.

\subsection{Contact Embracing}
The fabric sensor is more sensitive and has virtually no minimum detectable force, making it ideal for contact avoidance tasks where the robot needs to react to light contacts and avoid collisions. We demonstrate this in a contact avoidance task, where the robot reacts to unexpected contacts while maintaining its primary end-effector goal. In this task, the robot uses tactile feedback from the fabric sensor to detect contact and generate repulsive forces in the null space of the IK optimization, allowing it use redundant degrees of freedom to move away from contact while still achieving its end-effector target. 

This task is possible with the TPU sensor as well, but the higher threshold forces and non-uniform geometry of the TPU sensor make it more difficult to reliably detect light contacts across the surface, which is important for effective contact avoidance.
