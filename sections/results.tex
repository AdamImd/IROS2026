\section{Real World Use Cases}
Given the characterization results, our configuration of the AM sensor is more suitable for contact embracing tasks that require higher forces and highly durable materials, while the textile sensor is more suitable for contact avoidance tasks that require higher sensitivity and lower threshold forces. 

We use our sensors to perform a variety of whole body contact manipulation tasks on the Boston Dynamics Spot platform. We developed a custom contact-aware whole body inverse kinematics algorithm that incorporates tactile feedback from our sensors to enable both contact avoidance and contact embracing behaviors during manipulation.

\subsection{Contact Embracing}
We demonstrate the ability of the AM sensor to enable contact with rigid objects in the environment through a contact embracing task, where the robot uses tactile feedback to gently open a door while balancing a dustpan in the gripper. We create a new end effector on the arm of the robot, and move along its normal until a force above a threshold is achieved. We also optimize the rotation angle of the gripper to prevent the dustpan from tipping over.

This task has a higher force range and requires the robot to actively seek contact with the door, while sensing when the door is fully open and maintaining a desired contact force.

\subsection{Contact Avoidance}
The textile sensor is more sensitive and has virtually no minimum detectable force, making it ideal for contact avoidance tasks where the robot needs to react to light contacts and avoid collisions. We demonstrate this in a contact avoidance task, where the robot reacts to unexpected contacts while maintaining its primary end-effector goal. In this task, the robot uses tactile feedback from the textile sensor to detect contact and optimize the inverse kinematics solution to move away from the contact while still maintaining the end-effector XYZ position.
