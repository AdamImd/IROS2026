Here we describe the sensor designs for the two architectures, tailored to the arm links of the Spot mobile manipulation platform. We design them to balance three practical requirements: (1) operating force range, (2) sensing repeatability across repeated load cycles, and (3) deployability on commercial robots with minimal hardware modification.

Both sensor designs share a common operating principle: a matrix of conductive rows and columns separated by a piezoresistive layer (Velostat).When contact occurs at a taxel, the piezoresistive layer compresses and becomes more conductive, changing the resistance at the row–column intersection, which is measured by a microcontroller through a voltage divider circuit. Both designs are optimized for maximal surface coverage, analog force readings across individual taxels, and low-cost accessible fabrication, and are adaptable to most robot geometries.

We implement two complementary variants: (1) a textile-based sensor using conductive fabric layers, and (2) an additive manufacturing (AM) based sensor with 3D-printed conformal shells and copper-tape electrodes. We chose design variables such that the textile sensor prioritizes flexibility and sensitivity at lower force ranges, while the AM sensor provides conformal solution suited to higher force ranges. In these configurations, they cover the force-sensing range needed for diverse mobile manipulation tasks. We describe the design and fabrication of each in the following sections, with an overview shown in Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}.

\subsection{Textile Sensor Design}
The textile sensor shown in Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both} is designed to be flexible, conforming naturally to the robot arm without rigid shells. Both the textile and AM sensor use a matrix of conductive rows and columns separated by a piezoresistive Velostat layer, with the same taxel pitch of approximately \textit{20mm}. The sensor is formed into a cylindrical sleeve using hook-and-loop tape as a closure, allowing it to be easily wrapped around and removed from the robot link for maintenance. Rows and columns are interfaced with the microcontroller via snap buttons.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Inner layer:} The inner layer template is derived from a 2D projection of the robot link geometry and cut from a textured off the shelf fabric, chosen for its flexibility and high friction with the robot surface to prevent slipping against the robot surface. Rows of conductive fabric are adhered to the inner layer, with excess fabric removed at taxel boundaries to electrically isolate adjacent rows.
\item \textbf{Outer layer:} The outer layer uses the same template and also uses off the shelf fabric, providing a soft contact surface for improved sensitivity. Columns of conductive fabric are adhered to the outer layer, with excess fabric removed at taxel boundaries to isolate adjacent columns.
\item \textbf{Force Sensitive Layer:} Velostat squares are cut such that they are slightly wider than the conductive rows and columns to prevent short-circuiting.  
\item \textbf{Assembly:} The inner layer, Velostat squares, and outer layer are stacked in order and a fabric adhesive is used around each Velostat square to secure it. The sensor is then wrapped around the robot link, then secured with hook-and-loop tape. Snap buttons connect wires to each row and column, which are routed to a microcontroller.
\end{enumerate}
The fabrication of this sensor only requires an iron, and the average material cost for this sensor is approximately less than $\$10$ per arm link.

\subsection{Additive Manufacturing (AM) Sensor Design}
To design the 3D-printed skin, we start from the link-level mesh files provided by Boston Dynamics (Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-2, yellow) as part of their URDF models. For each link, we apply the following sequence of CAD operations:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Inner layer:} Generate a conformal shell with a uniform thickness of \textit{3mm} offset outward from the robot's base geometry (Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-2, green).
\item \textbf{Outer layer:} A second shell (\textit{3 mm} thick) offset \textit{5 mm} from the robot’s base geometry to accommodate the sensing stack (Fig.~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-2, orange).
\item \textbf{Ridges:} Within the inter-shell region, add \textit{1 mm} thick horizontal ridges on the inner shell and vertical ridges on the outer shell, each \textit{2mm} high. The spacing between adjacent ridges determines the taxel density (Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-2, cutout).
\item \textbf{Clearance cuts:} Material is removed near kinematic joints to prevent interference during motion, and a longitudinal cut is added along each shell to facilitate installation on the robot arm (Fig~\ref{fig:tactile_sensors_both}-1).
\item \textbf{Assembly:} Print shells in 95A TPU. Laminate Velostat with copper tape (conductive adhesive), adhere to the inner shell, and apply copper tape to the outer shell for opposing electrodes. Connect rows/columns to the microcontroller and secure the assembled shells to the robot arm.
\end{enumerate}


 The fabrication of this sensor requires a 3D printer capable of printing flexible filaments, and the average material cost also remains under \textit{\$10} per link.

\subsection{Design Variables}

Both architectures expose practical design variables that can be tuned to match task requirements for force range, repeatability, and fabrication effort.

If a task only requires contact/no-contact detection, the Velostat layer can be omitted to simplify fabrication and wiring. For continuous force sensing, however, the Velostat layer is retained and the variables below are tuned to shape the sensing response.

For the textile sensor, the dominant variables are outer-layer material stiffness/thickness and the inclusion of an optional spacer material. Softer or thinner outer layers improve low-force sensitivity. Conversely, thicker and stiffer fabrics shift the sensing range upward. To expand the dynamic range, a spacer material (e.g., foam) can be inserted between the outer fabric and the electrodes; removing it narrows the range for specialized high-precision applications. Furthermore, hysteresis is minimized by utilizing stiffer outer fabrics and minimizing or eliminating the spacer material. A practical downside of the textile sensor is the mechanical coupling during installation: it is difficult to attach it to the robot with consistent tension, leading to variability in the sensor’s characteristic curve across different deployments.

For the AM sensor, the dominant variables are shell thickness, ridge height, ridge spacing, and print infill density. To shift the sensing range downward for higher sensitivity, one can decrease the shell thickness, utilize softer TPU-like materials, decrease infill density, or lower the ridge height. To shift the range upward, these parameters are reversed: increasing ridge height, material stiffness, and infill density provides higher resistance to deformation. Expanding the force range is achieved by increasing outer material thickness or adding a spacer, while narrowing it involves thinner/softer materials combined with higher infill density. Hysteresis in the AM sensor—often a byproduct of the material's viscoelasticity, and a function of the infill density and outer shell stiffness. The AM sensor's design allows for more consistent installation due to its rigid-conformal nature, though it is susceptible to performance variance stemming from infill geometry and 3D-printing artifacts.

While textile and AM sensors share the same operating principle, the design variables differ in their physical manifestation and practical tuning. The textile sensor operates with its conductive layers in contact with the Velostat during normal operation, whereas the AM sensor utilizes a designed gap between the conductive layers and the Velostat, which is only closed upon mechanical compression. This structural difference allows for complementary behavioral profiles: the textile sensor is ideal for high-sensitivity contact avoidance and light-touch HRI, while the AM sensor is optimized for contact-embracing tasks that require a high binary threshold and durability under significant loads, such as bracing against a wall or opening heavy doors.

In terms of cross-talk, the textile sensor is more susceptible due to the continuous contact between layers, which results in a lower baseline resistance across the entire matrix. In contrast, the AM sensor’s "normally open" gap ensures most cells maintain nearly infinite baseline resistance. This architectural choice significantly reduces cross-talk and increases the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), allowing for reliable force localization using simpler electronics without the need for intensive software-side calibration.
